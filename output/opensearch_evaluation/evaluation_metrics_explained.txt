Evaluation Metrics And Result Interpretation
============================================

This project evaluates OpenSearch hybrid retrieval with leave-one-out testing.
For each record, the evaluator uses that record as a query, retrieves top-k
results, removes the query record itself, and scores the remaining results
against a relevance rule.


Evaluation Setups
-----------------

1. no-MSC evaluation

   Data:
   - output/embeddings/no_msc_clean/works.jsonl
   - output/embeddings/no_msc_clean/embeddings.npy

   Relevance rule:
   - A result is relevant if it has the same ISSN as the query record.
   - If ISSN is missing, the evaluator falls back to exact normalized venue.

   What this measures:
   - Whether the system retrieves papers from the same journal/venue.
   - This is a rough proxy for journal recommendation quality.

2. MSC evaluation

   Data:
   - output/embeddings/msc_clean/works.jsonl
   - output/embeddings/msc_clean/embeddings.npy

   Default relevance rule:
   - A result is relevant if it shares at least one top-level MSC class with
     the query record.
   - Example: 17B56 and 17B37 are relevant to each other because both are
     top-level MSC class 17.

   What this measures:
   - Whether the system retrieves papers from the same broad mathematical
     subject area.
   - This is a stronger topic-relevance evaluation than the no-MSC venue proxy.


Hybrid Scenarios Evaluated
--------------------------

Two hybrid score settings were evaluated:

1. hybrid_50_50
   - BM25 weight: 0.5
   - Dense vector weight: 0.5

2. hybrid_70_30
   - BM25 weight: 0.7
   - Dense vector weight: 0.3

Both scenarios produced identical aggregate scores in the current runs. That
suggests the two weight settings are not changing the final ranking under the
current OpenSearch normalization/candidate setup, or that both BM25 and dense
retrieval are returning very similar top candidates.


Metrics
-------

1. precision@10

   Formula:
   relevant results in top 10 / 10

   Meaning:
   - Measures how many of the first 10 recommendations are relevant.
   - This is one of the most important recommender metrics because users
     usually inspect only the top results.

   Example:
   precision@10 = 0.80 means 8 out of the top 10 results are relevant.


2. recall@10

   Formula:
   relevant results in top 10 / total relevant results in the corpus

   Meaning:
   - Measures how much of the full relevant set is recovered in the top 10.
   - This can look low when there are many relevant documents in the corpus.

   Important:
   - Low recall@10 does not always mean bad retrieval.
   - If a query has 200 relevant records in the corpus, even a perfect top 10
     can only reach recall@10 = 10 / 200 = 0.05.


3. MRR@10

   Full name:
   mean reciprocal rank at 10

   Formula:
   1 / rank of the first relevant result

   Meaning:
   - Measures how quickly the first relevant result appears.
   - If the first relevant result is rank 1, reciprocal rank is 1.0.
   - If the first relevant result is rank 5, reciprocal rank is 0.2.
   - If no relevant result appears in top 10, reciprocal rank is 0.0.

   Why it matters:
   - Good for checking whether the system gives at least one strong useful
     result near the top.


4. nDCG@10

   Full name:
   normalized discounted cumulative gain at 10

   Meaning:
   - Measures ranking quality across the top 10.
   - Relevant results near rank 1 count more than relevant results near rank 10.
   - The score is normalized between 0 and 1.

   Why it matters:
   - A system that puts relevant results at ranks 1, 2, and 3 is better than a
     system that puts them at ranks 8, 9, and 10, even if both have the same
     precision@10.


Observed Results
----------------

1. no-MSC evaluation

   Queries evaluated: 613
   Relevance: same ISSN, falling back to same venue

   hybrid_50_50:
   - precision@10 = 0.1688
   - recall@10    = 0.1641
   - MRR@10       = 0.3368
   - nDCG@10      = 0.2389

   hybrid_70_30:
   - precision@10 = 0.1688
   - recall@10    = 0.1641
   - MRR@10       = 0.3368
   - nDCG@10      = 0.2389

   Interpretation:
   - precision@10 = 0.1688 means only about 1.7 out of the top 10 results are
     same-journal/same-venue matches.
   - MRR@10 = 0.3368 means the first relevant result often appears around rank
     3 when it appears, but many queries still have weak top-10 results.
   - nDCG@10 = 0.2389 indicates weak-to-moderate ranking quality.

   Assessment:
   - This is a useful baseline, but not a strong final journal recommender.
   - The no-MSC relevance rule is strict and venue-oriented. A semantically
     useful paper from a different journal is counted as non-relevant, so the
     metric may underestimate subject similarity.


2. MSC evaluation

   Queries evaluated: 2097
   Relevance: shared top-level MSC class

   hybrid_50_50:
   - precision@10 = 0.8579
   - recall@10    = 0.0502
   - MRR@10       = 0.9679
   - nDCG@10      = 0.8792

   hybrid_70_30:
   - precision@10 = 0.8579
   - recall@10    = 0.0502
   - MRR@10       = 0.9679
   - nDCG@10      = 0.8792

   Interpretation:
   - precision@10 = 0.8579 means about 8.6 out of the top 10 results share the
     same broad MSC subject area.
   - MRR@10 = 0.9679 means the first relevant result is almost always at rank 1.
   - nDCG@10 = 0.8792 means relevant results are not only present, but ranked
     very high.
   - recall@10 = 0.0502 looks low because the top-level MSC relevance set is
     broad. Many queries have a large number of relevant records in the corpus,
     so top 10 can only recover a small fraction.

   Assessment:
   - The MSC pipeline is strong as a subject-area recommender.
   - The low recall@10 is justified by the broad relevance set and small top-k.
   - For this workflow, precision@10, MRR@10, and nDCG@10 are more meaningful
     than recall@10.


Why MSC Results Are Much Better Than no-MSC Results
---------------------------------------------------

MSC embeddings include:
- title
- MSC codes
- MSC descriptions
- venue
- abstract

no-MSC embeddings include:
- title
- venue
- abstract

The MSC workflow has explicit subject labels and subject descriptions inside
the embedded text. Therefore, it is much easier for both BM25 and dense vector
search to retrieve papers from the same mathematical area.

The no-MSC workflow is evaluated against journal/venue matching. That is a
harder and different target. Two papers can be topically similar but published
in different journals, and the no-MSC evaluator counts that as not relevant.


Overall Conclusion
------------------

The no-MSC pipeline is a weak-to-moderate baseline for same-journal retrieval.
It needs more tuning or better relevance signals before being treated as a
strong journal recommender.

The MSC pipeline is strong for retrieving papers from the same broad
mathematical subject area. Its high precision@10, MRR@10, and nDCG@10 justify
the quality of the top-ranked recommendations. Its low recall@10 is expected
because many papers share the same top-level MSC class, while evaluation only
looks at the top 10 results.

Recommended next metrics:
- hit-rate@10: whether at least one relevant result appears in top 10
- recall@50 or recall@100: more meaningful when the relevant set is large
- exact-MSC evaluation: stricter than top-level MSC relevance
- separate keyword-only and vector-only baselines: shows whether hybrid improves
  over each branch independently
